Nitrogen and Sulfur Cycling on the Early Earth

As part of the Anderson lab group at Carleton College, I have worked on various projects that use publicly availible genomes to learn about microbes on the early earth. We recently wrapped up a project where we examined the history of various genes required for nitrogen cycling using bioinformatic methods (preprint here). We also plan to investigate sulfur cycling genes to determine when certain metabolisms were particularly adventageous in the history of the early Earth. The findings of these studies may have implications to find extraterrestrial life, and this work is done in cooperation with the Virtual Planetary Lab at UW.

Sulfur Biogeochemical Cycling by Shewanella BF02

I was part of the Fort Johnson REU program at the College of Charleston during the summer of 2019. I worked with Dr. Peter Lee at the Hollings Marine Laboratory. I focused on a strain of Shewanella isolated from the outflow of Blood Falls, Antarctica. I was broadly interested in the potential roles of Shewanella in the sulfur cylce. I found evidence that Shewanella is able to produce dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), a volatile organic sulfur compound important in global temperature regulation and that Shewanella preferentially coupled specific sulfur sources with iron redox cycling.
